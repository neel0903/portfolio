The English merchants would have been glad to see him expel once and\r\nfor all the tenants of the Steelyard, but he never liked to commit\r\nhimself to a position from which there was no retreat; and he could not\r\nforget that the Hansa, driven to desperation, would be a formidable\r\nenemy. Their\r\ncompetition was not so vital to English interests, and there were\r\npolitical reasons for dealing with them in a more civil manner.\r\nHarshness and insolence could not be displayed towards Spain, since the\r\nmatrimonial alliance with that country was the keynote of Henryâ\x80\x99s\r\npolicy, to which mercantile considerations had necessarily to be\r\nsubordinate. He was certainly fortunate in the choice which the Spanish\r\nsovereigns made of a representative in England.\r\n\r\nDr. de Puebla, who filled that office during the greater part of his\r\nreign, was a mean and venal figure, amenable alike to flattery,\r\nbullying, and bribes; and the king was able to read him like a book and\r\nplay upon all his weaknesses in turn. Here the cargoes of Levant wines,\r\nsilks, spices, and other eastern goods were disposed of, while the crews\r\ndispersed over the country to hawk the petty merchandises of their own\r\nwhich they were allowed to carry on board. The usual time taken was twelve months or a little longer. The\r\nFlanders galleys first sailed in 1517, and their last voyage was in\r\n1552; towards the end of this period the sailings became very irregular,\r\nowing to wars in Italy and the gradual decline of the old trade\r\nroutes.[42]\r\n\r\nBefore the close of the epoch now under discussion the great\r\ngeographical discoveries which ushered in the oceanic era of commerce\r\nbegan to make their effects evident.
